# Bloom — Customer Discovery Interviews

**Product:** Bloom — a web app for people who buy or receive cut flowers. Scan your bouquet, identify each flower, get a lifespan estimate, and receive simple care tips to maximize it.

**Interview method:** The Mom Test (Rob Fitzpatrick)
**Core rules applied:**
- Talk about their life, not your idea
- Ask about specifics in the past, not hypotheticals about the future
- Talk less, listen more
- Deflect compliments, anchor fluff, dig beneath surface answers

**Scary assumptions being tested:**
- People don't actually try to keep flowers alive longer
- Most people just let flowers die without caring

---

## Interview 1: Maya, 26, Grad Student

**Context:** Casual coffee shop setting. You know Maya through a mutual friend. You mentioned you wanted to chat about her grocery shopping habits.

---

**You:** So you mentioned you grab flowers pretty often. When did you last pick some up?

**Maya:** Oh, like three days ago actually. I got this little mixed bouquet from Trader Joe's. It's on my desk right now.

**You:** Nice. What made you grab that one specifically?

**Maya:** Honestly? It was the cheapest one that didn't look sad. I think it was like $6.99. I liked that it had some purple in it.

**You:** Do you know what flowers were in it?

**Maya:** *(laughs)* No idea. There's like... something fluffy and purple, and then some white ones? I genuinely could not tell you.

**You:** Has that ever been an issue for you, not knowing what they are?

**Maya:** I mean, not really an issue. I just pick by vibe. But sometimes I'll see something at the store and I'm like, what even is that, is it good, will it last? And I just... guess.

> **Mom Test note:** She's not saying the names are a blocker. She's saying she picks by vibe. Don't project your pain onto her yet. Keep digging.

**You:** Talk me through what happens after you get them home.

**Maya:** I put them in a vase, change the water maybe once? And then I just watch them slowly die and feel bad about it.

**You:** Feel bad how?

**Maya:** Like I wasted money. I paid $7 and they lasted four days. I feel like I did something wrong but I don't know what.

**You:** Have you ever tried to figure out what you did wrong?

**Maya:** Yeah actually. I Googled "how to make flowers last longer" like two weeks ago after a bouquet died really fast. I found something about cutting the stems at an angle and putting them away from sunlight.

**You:** Did you do it?

**Maya:** I cut the stems, yeah. I forgot about the sunlight thing until right now.

> **Mom Test note:** She searched. She took partial action. This is a real signal. She cares enough to look it up but the information didn't stick or translate into full behavior change.

**You:** What was it like trying to find that information?

**Maya:** It was fine, I just Googled it. But the article was kind of long and had a lot of specific stuff like "for roses do this, for tulips do that" and I didn't actually know what I had so I just did the general tips and hoped for the best.

**You:** That's interesting. So the advice existed but wasn't quite usable for you?

**Maya:** Yeah. Like it assumed I knew what I was working with.

**You:** Has that happened more than once, looking something up and hitting that same wall?

**Maya:** Probably every time, honestly. I never know what I have.

**You:** What would you have done if you couldn't find anything on Google?

**Maya:** Probably just let them die. Which is kind of what I do anyway because I forget to follow through.

> **Mom Test note:** Honest admission. The problem is real but her current coping mechanism is "accept the loss." The question is whether she'd change behavior with a lower-friction solution, or whether she's fine with the current outcome.

**You:** What's the longest a bouquet has lasted you?

**Maya:** Maybe ten days? That felt like a win. Usually it's like five or six.

**You:** Last thing: is there anyone else you know who buys flowers regularly that I should talk to?

**Maya:** My roommate does, she gets them almost every week. She's way more into it than me — she'd probably have a lot to say.

## Interview 2: Marcus, 38, Dad of Two

**Context:** Neighborhood cookout. Marcus mentioned earlier he just came from picking up flowers for the kitchen table.

---

**You:** You mentioned you just grabbed flowers today. Is that a regular thing for you?

**Marcus:** Yeah, pretty regular. Every couple weeks at least. Sometimes more if there's something going on — anniversary, someone's birthday.

**You:** What'd you get today?

**Marcus:** Sunflowers and some pink ones. I don't know what they're called. They're kind of round and layered.

**You:** Walk me through how you pick them. Like what goes through your head at the store?

**Marcus:** Honestly I go right to what I know. Sunflowers, tulips, those round pink ones. I don't mess around with anything else because I've grabbed random stuff before and it looked bad in the vase or died in two days and I felt dumb.

**You:** Tell me about a time that happened.

**Marcus:** Yeah, maybe a year ago I grabbed this bouquet that had a bunch of different stuff in it because it looked impressive. Got home, put it in water, and within like three days half of it was dead. The rest lasted a week. My wife was like "what did you get?" and I didn't even know what to tell her.

**You:** What did you do after that?

**Marcus:** Nothing really. Just went back to what I know works.

> **Mom Test note:** He tried something, it failed, and he retreated. He did NOT search for a solution. He adapted his behavior by narrowing his choices. This is the scary assumption showing up. He's not trying to solve the underlying problem. He found a workaround.

**You:** Did you ever try to figure out why that bouquet died fast?

**Marcus:** No. I figured some flowers just don't last. I didn't really think there was something I could have done differently.

**You:** Has it ever bothered you, not knowing what you're looking at in the flower section?

**Marcus:** I mean, a little. Sometimes I want to get something different, surprise my wife with something she hasn't seen before. But I don't want to gamble on it. So I just don't.

**You:** What would it take for you to feel confident enough to grab something new?

**Marcus:** I'd probably need to know it was going to last and that it looked decent together. Like if someone I trusted said "this one's good, grab it," I'd go for it.

> **Mom Test note:** He's describing a trusted recommendation, not a tool or app. Don't jump to conclusions, but note that his mental model for solving this is a person, not a product.

**You:** Has anyone ever played that role for you? Florist, friend, anyone?

**Marcus:** My wife knows more than I do. Sometimes I'll text her a photo of something and ask if it's nice. But she's not always on her phone.

**You:** What do you do when she doesn't respond?

**Marcus:** I just grab the usual.

**You:** How often would you say you end up with the safe picks versus something new?

**Marcus:** Safe picks probably 90% of the time. Maybe more.

**You:** One more: who else do you know who buys flowers a lot that might be worth me talking to?

**Marcus:** Honestly my neighbor Linda. She's got flowers everywhere in her house, she probably knows way more than I do.

## Interview 3: Priya, 34, Manages the Household

**Context:** You met Priya through a local community group. She invited you over for tea. There are two arrangements visible in the living room.

---

**You:** These arrangements are really beautiful. How long have you had them?

**Priya:** The one by the window is from Monday so about five days. The one on the table I redid yesterday, those are fresh.

**You:** You redo them often?

**Priya:** Every week, sometimes more. It depends on what's available and what season we're in. Right now I'm doing a lot of peach and terracotta tones for fall.

**You:** When you say what's available, where do you shop?

**Priya:** I go to the farmers market Saturday mornings mostly. Sometimes a local florist if I need something specific. I try not to do grocery store bouquets because the quality is inconsistent.

**You:** How do you decide what to get at the farmers market?

**Priya:** I know most of the vendors now. I know which stall has the best dahlias, who brings the freshest eucalyptus. It took probably a year of going before I felt like I knew what I was doing.

> **Mom Test note:** She built expertise over time through repeated exposure. Her current state is competent. The question is whether she still has unsolved problems, or whether she's past the pain you're solving for.

**You:** Was there a time before that year, when you were still figuring it out, that was frustrating?

**Priya:** Oh, very much so. I used to buy things that died in two days and I had no idea why. I'd buy a whole bunch of something and it just wouldn't open, or it would wilt immediately. I was putting money into it and not getting the result.

**You:** What did you do about it?

**Priya:** I watched a lot of YouTube. There's a few channels I still follow actually. And I asked one of the vendors at the market to teach me what to look for when buying, like how to tell if something is fresh versus already past its peak.

**You:** That's really specific. Do you remember what pushed you to go looking for that information?

**Priya:** I spent probably $40 at the market one week and by Thursday everything was drooping. I was so frustrated. I sat down that evening and just started researching everything.

**You:** $40 is real money. What did you find when you started looking?

**Priya:** A lot of it was scattered. Like I'd find a blog post about roses specifically, or a video about conditioning stems, but nothing that put it all together for someone who was just starting. I kind of had to piece it together myself.

**You:** How long did that take?

**Priya:** Honestly months before I felt confident. And I'm still learning. Last month I tried ranunculus for the first time and completely butchered the care. They lasted three days.

> **Mom Test note:** Even an experienced user still hits the new variety problem. This is a meaningful signal. The pain doesn't fully go away with expertise. It recurs every time you try something unfamiliar.

**You:** What did you do when the ranunculus died?

**Priya:** Looked it up after the fact, which was too late obviously. Found out they need cold water, not room temperature. I didn't know that. Now I know.

**You:** Is looking things up after the fact a pattern for you?

**Priya:** *(pauses)* Yeah, actually. I usually find out what I did wrong after the damage is done. I don't usually research before buying something new, I just go for it and learn from the mistake.

**You:** Has that ever made you hesitant to try new flowers?

**Priya:** Sometimes. There are things I've been curious about for a while that I keep putting off because I don't want to waste them. Like I've never worked with sweet peas. I want to but I keep thinking I'll kill them.

> **Mom Test note:** Strong signal. She's self-limiting her exploration because of fear of failure and wasted cost. She is engaged, motivated, and willing to invest in solving this. Completely different profile from Marcus.

**You:** If you could know going in, before you buy something new, what it needs and roughly how forgiving it is, would that change anything?

**Priya:** That would change a lot actually. I'd experiment way more.

> **Mom Test note:** This is close to fluff. She's making a future-tense claim. Don't bank on it. But combined with the fact that she already sought information, paid for flowers, and adjusted behavior, it's a stronger signal than the same words from someone who hasn't done any of that.

**You:** Last thing: who else should I be talking to about this?

**Priya:** There's a woman in my community group, Diane, who just started getting into flowers. She's exactly where I was two years ago. She'd probably be more useful to you honestly, she's still in that frustrating early phase.

## Summary of Key Signals Across All Three Interviews

| Signal | Maya | Marcus | Priya |
|---|---|---|---|
| Has searched for flower care info | Yes | No | Yes, extensively |
| Took action on what they found | Partial | No | Yes |
| Feels the financial sting of waste | Yes | Somewhat | Yes |
| Self-limiting behavior due to uncertainty | Somewhat | Strongly | Somewhat |
| Would change behavior with better info up front | Unclear | Unclear | Likely yes |
| Has an existing workaround | Sort of (general Googling) | Yes (same 3 flowers always) | Yes (vendor relationships, YouTube) |

---

## The Uncomfortable Finding

Marcus is the profile most likely to represent a large chunk of casual buyers, and he has zero motivation to seek out a solution. He worked around the problem by narrowing his choices. If Bloom's target user is Marcus, you have a market risk problem.

If it's Maya or Priya, the signal is much warmer. Maya represents the low-engagement but cost-conscious buyer who has tried and failed to find usable information. Priya represents the motivated buyer who still hits friction every time she encounters a new variety. Both have demonstrated they care enough to search. Marcus has not.

**Next 3 big questions to answer:**
1. How many buyers behave like Marcus versus Maya? If Marcus is the majority, the market may not pull hard enough for a solution.
2. Does the friction Priya describes at the "new variety" moment happen often enough to anchor repeat usage, or is it a one-time onboarding problem?
3. Is the scan-and-identify feature the hook that gets people in the door, or is the lifespan estimate the thing they actually care about?
